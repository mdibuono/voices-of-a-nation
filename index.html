What is this?

We digitised, cleaned, and structured over 460,000 individual speeches from the official stenographic records of the Italian Chamber of Deputies — and linked each one to the person who spoke it.

Between 1900 and 1924, Italy’s parliament debated war and peace, labour rights, colonial ambitions, universal suffrage, and political violence. These are the actual words of the people who shaped modern Italy — socialists, liberals, nationalists, and fascists arguing on the floor of the Chamber.

6 August 1900 · Legislature 21 · On the assassination of King Umberto I
Filippo Turati
“Onorevoli colleghi, siamo qui per protestare, concordi insieme con voi, contro un fatto che ha sollevato la coscienza pubblica, che solleva la nostra. L'omicidio politico, nelle presenti condizioni dei paesi civili, colpisca un Re, un Presidente di Repubblica, un qualsiasi legittimo rappresentante d'interessi collettivi, od anche un semplice portabandiera d'un ideale novatore, è anche più altamente riprovevole, perché rispecchia un concetto antiquato, barbarico della evoluzione sociale; esso suppone che la distruzione di uno, o di più uomini, muti qualcosa alla sostanza di un assetto politico o sociale. Associandoci al vostro cordoglio, noi qui rinnoviamo l'augurio, che dal delitto d'un forsennato nessun pregiudizio debba patire la libertà del paese.”
The socialist leader condemned the anarchist assassination of the king while warning against any crackdown on civil liberties. Deputies on the right shouted “Basta! basta!”

Why This Corpus

The questions behind the data.

Why this corpus exists

Democracies don’t collapse in a day. They erode — through shifts in language, in who gets to speak, in what can and cannot be said. The erosion is slow enough that contemporaries often miss it. But it leaves traces.

Parliamentary speech is one of the richest traces available. Every intervention is recorded, dated, and attributed. It captures not just policy positions but emotional registers, rhetorical strategies, and the evolving norms of political discourse. When a deputy chooses the word patria over nazione, or interrupts a colleague, or stays silent during a debate on political violence — these are data points.

Italy between 1900 and 1924 offers an extraordinary setting to study these dynamics. In this single period, the country experienced a massive expansion of suffrage, a world war, the rise of mass politics, and the destruction of parliamentary democracy. Until now, no structured dataset made it possible to study this transformation systematically. This corpus fills that gap.

What this data makes possible

Measuring democratic erosion in real time. Can we detect the collapse of parliamentary norms — rising polarisation, declining deliberation, the silencing of opposition — in the text itself, before the institutional breakdown?

Tracing how historical violence shapes political rhetoric. Deputies who grew up in provinces marked by the coercive unification of Italy carry that experience into the Chamber. Do they speak about the nation differently?

Understanding how democratisation changes elite discourse. When the electorate triples overnight in 1912, representatives face a fundamentally different audience. How — and how quickly — does their language adapt?

Building systematic evidence on topics that have only been studied anecdotally. Women’s rights, colonial policy, labour conflict, emigration, the Southern Question — the full text of what was said, by whom, and when.

These questions span economics, political science, history, sociology, linguistics, or simply general interest. The corpus provides a common empirical foundation for all of them.

Two ways to read a speech

Every deputy in this corpus serves a dual analytical role — and this duality shapes our entire research agenda:

As a mirror of society

These men represent communities. Their words reflect the fears, values, and grievances of the people who elected them — including millions who left no written record of their own.

As a strategic actor

Deputies respond to incentives. When the electorate expands, they adapt their rhetoric. When political violence escalates, they choose to denounce, accommodate, or stay silent. Their choices reveal how democratic institutions are defended — or abandoned — from within.

ⓘ Methodology

The rise and fall of “patria”

How often do deputies invoke patria (fatherland), libertà (liberty), nazione (nation), or popolo (the people)? The language of national identity triples during WWI, then collapses. “Libertà” surges in 1919 as democracy expands — and again in 1923, this time in desperation.

How They Talked

The emotions, conflicts, and silences in parliamentary debate.

The emotional temperature

The stenographers wrote down every audience reaction: (Applausi), (Interruzioni), (Rumori), (Si ride). Applause surges tenfold after 1918. Interruptions peak during the Biennio Rosso (1919–20). Parliament becomes a stage, then a battlefield.

ⓘ Methodology

The mood of parliament

We track three emotional registers across all speeches: anger (vergogna, scandalo, violenza, menzogna...), hope (progresso, giustizia, riforma, civiltà...), and fear (pericolo, minaccia, anarchia, rivoluzione...). Fear overtakes hope after 1919.

The average age of speakers drops 13 years between 1918 and 1923. After WWI, young veterans flood the Chamber with radicalism and impatience. Mussolini is 38 when he becomes Prime Minister.

Curiosities

The unexpected things you find in half a million speeches.

Who spoke the most words?

Excluding the Chamber president (who speaks procedurally), Filippo Turati spoke nearly one million words in parliament — the equivalent of 10 novels. Giolitti: 700,000. Nitti: 480,000.

ⓘ Methodology

Who gets mentioned most?

Not who speaks, but who gets talked about. Turati is mentioned in 5,000 speeches by other deputies. Garibaldi — dead since 1882 — is still invoked 1,010 times. Even Bismarck shows up 161 times.

ⓘ Methodology

The longest speech

13,125 words by Ettore Reina (1 August 1920) — nearly a novella, in one session during the Biennio Rosso.

The shortest

“Ma io qualche volta ci sono stato!” — Piero Foscari, 34 characters, 9 September 1919.

Half of all speeches are under 30 words

48.6% of interventions are under 200 characters. Most parliamentary speech isn’t grand oratory — it’s procedural: “Ai voti!”, “Ha facoltà di parlare”, “La seduta è tolta.” The long speeches — over 5,000 characters — are only 3.6% of the total, but they contain the real debates.

They still spoke Latin

Italian deputies in 1900–1923 used Latin phrases routinely: a priori (803 times), modus vivendi (209), sine qua non (115), de jure (109), ad hoc (100). The classical education of the Italian elite was alive and well.

Violence and Nation-Building in Elite Rhetoric: Evidence from Italian Parliamentary Debates, 1900–1924

Maurizia Di Buono, Sergio Galletta, Andrea Geraci, Gianluca Mazzarella

Click to read abstract ↓

This paper studies whether exposure to violence during state formation shapes the nation-building rhetoric of political elites, and whether democratisation changes how such formative experiences are expressed in national politics. We assemble a new corpus of speeches from the Italian Chamber of Deputies between 1900 and 1924 and link each intervention to deputies’ biographical characteristics. To capture early-life exposure to coercive state formation, we merge deputies’ places and cohorts of origin to fine-grained measures of political violence associated with Italian unification and its aftermath, focusing on the “Great Brigandage” and related repression. We measure nation-building rhetoric using a text-as-data approach that combines pairwise comparisons of excerpts with Bradley–Terry scaling and supervised prediction. Our empirical strategy exploits the 1912 suffrage reform, which expanded the electorate sharply while leaving electoral districts unchanged, to study how the rhetoric of deputies from more violent origins adjusts when political representation broadens.

How It Was Built

From century-old printed pages to a structured digital corpus, in five stages.

Original documents

The starting point is the official Atti Parlamentari: scanned PDF copies of the original printed stenographic reports from the Italian Parliament’s historical archive. One PDF per parliamentary session, with double-column layouts, complex formatting, and inconsistent headers.

PDF to images

Each PDF page is converted into an individual image. Filenames encode the legislature, session number, and date (e.g. leg_r24_sed001_19131128_p0001.png). When automatic date extraction fails, dates are corrected manually.

AI-powered text extraction

An AI language model reads each page image and extracts structured speech data: who is speaking, their institutional role, and the full text of what they said. Standard text extraction tools couldn’t handle the historical double-column layout and embedded speaker transitions, so we used a custom approach with a large language model.

Post-processing & cleaning

Automated checks catch extraction errors. Written responses (marked “RISPOSTA SCRITTA”) are matched to their actual author by detecting the signature at the end of the text. Sessions with parsing failures are re-processed. Speeches are then cleaned, merged, and deduplicated across a 7-phase pipeline.

Deputy database & linking

Personal information about each deputy is assembled from 5 independent sources (official Camera database, Wikipedia, Wikidata, storia.camera.it, and ISTAT geographic data). Each speech is then matched to its speaker — handling name variants, compound surnames, and people who share the same last name. Details in Linking Speeches.

Linking Speeches to People

Names can be misspelled by OCR, some deputies share the same surname, and some speakers aren’t even deputies. Four steps handle this, and if any step is ambiguous, the speech stays unmatched — never assigned to the wrong person.

1

Full name

“Sonnino Sidney” → matches by surname + first name, resolving shared surnames.

2

Unique surname

“Giolitti” → only one in that legislature. Safe match.

3

Compound surname

“Di Scalea” matches “Lanza di Scalea” via separate lookup.

4

Fuzzy

Normalise accents, hyphens, OCR variants. Always homonym-safe.
